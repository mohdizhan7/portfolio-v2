Most people treat UAT as a quality gate. Run the test scenarios, log the defects, sign off the document, go live.

I treat it as the last design phase.

This distinction changes everything — how you structure the sessions, who you involve, what you do with what you find, and how you measure whether it worked.

## The Standard Approach and Its Failure Mode

The standard UAT approach goes like this: developers build the system, business analysts write test scripts, end users execute the scripts, defects get logged, critical ones get fixed, the rest get deferred, sign-off happens, go live.

The problem with this model is that it positions users as testers of a finished product. Their job is to find bugs, not to shape the system. Their feedback is filtered through a defect log. Design decisions made six months ago are immovable because the build is done.

The result: systems that pass UAT and still fail in production. Not because of technical defects — because of design defects that no test script could have caught.

## What UAT Actually Surfaces

When I ran UAT for HUL Samadhan across three distribution hubs, I structured it differently. Each site got a two-week immersive session, not a script-execution exercise. Users ran live operations — real orders, real inventory, real pressure — with the new system alongside the old one.

What we found in week one of each site had nothing to do with defects. It was about workflow fit.

The system was technically correct. The picking sequence logic, the bin assignment, the sortation rules — all working as specified. But the way warehouse supervisors naturally wanted to manage exceptions — the moment a bin overflowed, or a SKU was misplaced — the system's exception handling flow didn't match their mental model.

No test script would have surfaced this. It required watching people use the system under real pressure and asking: "What are you trying to do right now?"

## Redesigning in UAT

We made 11 significant workflow adjustments during UAT. Not bug fixes — design changes. Exception screens were restructured. Confirmation flows were shortened. One entire sub-process was reordered because supervisors consistently tried to do step 3 before step 2.

Each change went back to the development team with a two-day turnaround SLA. By the end of UAT at each site, the system had been materially improved — not just validated.

**Go-live across all three sites ran without a single day of downtime. Forty percent throughput increase. Zero operational regressions.**

## The Principle

UAT is the moment when real users interact with your design under real conditions for the first time. That moment contains more design information than any amount of upfront research.

The question is whether you've created a process that can receive that information and act on it — or whether you've created a process designed to conclude that everything is fine.

If your UAT produces zero design changes, one of two things is true: you designed a perfect system, or you designed a UAT process that couldn't hear what users were saying.

One of those is more likely.
